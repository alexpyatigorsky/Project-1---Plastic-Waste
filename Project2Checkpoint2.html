Research Question: Do wealthier countries produce more plastic pollution per capita?

When we directly compare GDP per capita to total plastic waste (per Fig. 1), we see a fairly uniform spread, suggesting that countries across the spectrum of wealth typically create a similar amount of pollution. There are angles to look at the cause of this, the most succinct being that countries with more wealth consume more on average, but also have better waste management systems. Poorer countries consume less on average but tend to have worse waste management systems.

However, we do see one heavy outlier, Switzerland, who has both a very high GDP per capita and easily the highest plastic waste per capita.

Three countries were omitted from the per capita visual: Taiwan, Montenegro, and “EMPTY”. Taiwan was removed for likely data collection issues since they recorded significantly more plastic pollution than multiple of the largest countries. Montenegro was removed for the same reason, an implausible amount of plastic pollution for their very small population. Cleanup events without a specified location could not be attributed to any country thus we removed “EMPTY”.

Why Switzerland?

We can clearly see Switzerland is an outlier in our dataset. They have a very large plastic pollution per capita value but notably also have a very high gdp per capita. We typically expect to see wealthier countries focus their resources in managing and recycling their waste.

We’re interested in diving deeper into Switzerland’s waste management systems and if it indicates why their pollution is so high.

Findings

Switzerland operates about 30 government waste incineration plants, as well as bans landfilling of combustible waste, so nearly all plastic is burned rather than recycled. We see high consumption directly in cleanup data instead of being recycled, which helps explain why their per capita number is so high.
